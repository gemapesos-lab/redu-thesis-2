# ThesisFormat-Chapters-1-3
ThesisFormat Chapters 1-3


### **THESIS TITLE**
- The title of the thesis provides a concise and clear summary of the research topic. It should reflect the main focus, purpose, and scope of the study.

---

### **APPROVAL AND ACCEPTANCE SHEET**
- This is a formal document signed by the thesis adviser(s), committee members, and sometimes the department head or dean. It signifies that the thesis has been reviewed, evaluated, and approved for submission and defense.

---

### **ACKNOWLEDGMENT**
- A section where the researcher expresses gratitude to individuals, institutions, or organizations that contributed to the completion of the thesis. This may include mentors, peers, family, funding agencies, etc.

---

### **TABLE OF CONTENTS**
- A structured outline of the thesis, listing all chapters, sections, subsections, and their corresponding page numbers. It helps readers navigate through the document easily.

---

### **LIST OF TABLES**
- A list of all tables included in the thesis, with their titles and page numbers. Tables are often used to present numerical data, comparisons, or summaries.

---

### **LIST OF FIGURES**
- Similar to the list of tables, this section lists all figures (e.g., graphs, charts, diagrams) used in the thesis, along with their titles and page numbers.

---

### **LIST OF ABBREVIATIONS**
- A compilation of abbreviations and acronyms used throughout the thesis, along with their full forms. This ensures clarity for readers unfamiliar with specific terms.

---

### **ABSTRACT**
- A brief summary (usually 150–250 words) of the entire thesis. It includes the research problem, objectives, methodology, key findings, and conclusions. The abstract provides a quick overview for readers.

---
### **CHAPTER 1**
### **INTRODUCTION**
The introduction sets the stage for the thesis and provides essential context for the research.

#### **Background of the Study**
- Describes the context and rationale for the research. It explains why the topic is important and relevant, often including historical, social, or scientific background information.

#### **Statement of the Problem**
- Clearly defines the research problem or gap in knowledge that the study aims to address. It outlines the specific issues the research seeks to solve.

#### **Significance of the Study**
- Explains the importance of the research and its potential contributions to the field, industry, or society. It highlights who will benefit from the study and how.

#### **Objectives**
- Lists the specific goals or aims of the research. These objectives guide the study and provide a framework for evaluating its success.

#### **Scope and Delimitations**
- Defines the boundaries of the study, specifying what is included and excluded. This clarifies the extent of the research and avoids overgeneralization.

#### **Conceptual Framework**
- A visual or textual representation of the relationships between variables or concepts in the study. It serves as a blueprint for understanding the research design.

#### **Theoretical Framework**
- Discusses the theories or models that underpin the study. It connects the research to existing academic knowledge and provides a foundation for the analysis.

#### **Definition of Terms**
- Provides clear definitions of key terms and concepts used in the thesis. This ensures consistency and avoids ambiguity in interpretation.

---
### **CHAPTER 2**
### **REVIEW OF RELATED LITERATURE**
- This chapter reviews existing studies, theories, and publications related to the research topic. It establishes the context for the study, identifies gaps in knowledge, and demonstrates how the current research builds on prior work.

### **CHAPTER 3**
### **METHODOLOGY**
This chapter details the methods and procedures used to conduct the research.

#### **Type of Research**
- Specifies whether the research is qualitative, quantitative, experimental, descriptive, etc.

#### **Research Design**
- Describes the overall structure and approach of the study. For example, it could be a case study, survey, experiment, or simulation.

#### **System Architecture**
- If applicable, this section outlines the technical architecture of the system being developed or studied. It may include diagrams or flowcharts.

#### **Hardware and Software Specifications**
- Lists the tools, technologies, and equipment used in the study, such as programming languages, databases, hardware components, etc.

#### **Method in Developing Software**
- Explains the process followed to develop the software or system, if applicable.

##### **Methodology Procedure**
- Details the steps taken during development.

###### **Agile Scrum**
- Describes the use of Agile methodologies, particularly Scrum, for iterative and collaborative development.

###### **Mobile Web Development**
- Focuses on techniques and tools used for developing mobile-friendly web applications.

#### **Method in Evaluation of the Study**
- Outlines how the study or system was tested and validated.

##### **Testing Method**
- Explains the testing procedures used to ensure the reliability and functionality of the system.

###### **Black Box Testing Procedure**
- Tests the system without knowledge of its internal workings, focusing on inputs and outputs.

###### **White Box Testing Procedure**
- Tests the system with knowledge of its internal structure, examining code logic and pathways.

#### **Data Collection Scheduling**
- Provides a timeline for when and how data were collected.

#### **Data Gathering**
- Describes the methods used to collect data, such as surveys, interviews, observations, or experiments.

#### **Respondents of the Study and Sampling Technique**
- Identifies the target population and explains how participants were selected (e.g., random sampling, purposive sampling).

#### **Statistical Treatment**
- Details the statistical methods used to analyze the data, such as mean, standard deviation, regression analysis, etc.

### **BIBLIOGRAPHY**
- A comprehensive list of all sources cited in the thesis, formatted according to a specific citation style (e.g., APA, MLA, Chicago). It demonstrates the depth of research and supports the credibility of the study.

### **General Guidelines for APA Bibliography**
1. **Title**: The bibliography should be titled **"References"** (centered, bold, at the top of the page).
2. **Ordering**: List entries alphabetically by the author's last name.
3. **Indentation**: Use a **hanging indent** (first line flush left, subsequent lines indented by 0.5 inches).
4. **Capitalization**: Use sentence case for titles of articles, books, and web pages (capitalize only the first word and proper nouns). For journal titles, use title case (capitalize all major words).
5. **Italics**: Italicize titles of books, journals, and standalone works.
6. **DOI or URL**: Include a DOI (Digital Object Identifier) if available. If no DOI is available, include a stable URL (without "Retrieved from" unless there’s a retrieval date).

# SMARTTASK: A Web application -  TASK-MANAGEMENT APPLICATION WITH INTELLIGENT REMINDER AND PRODUCTIVITY ANALYTICS

## CHAPTER 1
INTRODUCTION

### Background of the Study
The rapid advancement of computing has transformed how individuals manage daily activities, academic responsibilities, and professional workloads. Web applications have become essential tools for communication, information access, and personal organization. Among the most widely used applications are task-management systems, which assist users in tracking activities, deadlines, and priorities.

Despite the availability of numerous task-management applications, many users still experience difficulties such as missed deadlines, poor prioritization, and lack of awareness of their productivity patterns. Most existing applications rely on static reminders, requiring users to manually set alerts without considering user behavior, task urgency, or historical usage patterns.

This study proposes SmartTask, a web application task-management application that integrates intelligent reminder scheduling and productivity analytics to improve user task completion and time-management efficiency.

### Statement of the Problem
1. How effective are traditional task-management applications in helping users complete tasks on time?
2. How can an intelligent reminder system improve task completion rates?
3. How usable and user-friendly is an intelligent task-management application?
4. How can productivity analytics help users improve task-management behavior?
5. How effective is the proposed system compared to existing solutions?

### Objectives of the Study
**General Objective:**  
To design, develop, and evaluate a web application task-management application with intelligent reminders and analytics.

**Specific Objectives:**
1. Implement adaptive intelligent reminders.
2. Design a user-friendly interface.
3. Integrate productivity analytics.
4. Evaluate usability, performance, and effectiveness.
5. Compare the system with traditional applications.

### Significance of the Study
The study benefits students, professionals, developers, and researchers by providing an intelligent and data-driven productivity tool.

### Scope and Delimitations
The study focuses on web application and individual task management within a six-week evaluation period.

### Theoretical Framework

This study is anchored on Human–Computer Interaction (HCI) Theory, Behavioral Change Theory, and Adaptive Systems Theory, and is operationalized through algorithmic models from computer science.

- Human–Computer Interaction (HCI) Theory

	HCI theory guides the design of SmartTask to ensure usability, learnability, and reduced cognitive load. Algorithmic prioritization and scheduling techniques, such as priority scoring and Earliest Deadline First (EDF), limit information overload by surfacing only the most relevant tasks. Adaptive reminder timing further improves user experience by minimizing disruptive interactions.

- Behavioral Change Theory

	- Behavioral Change Theory supports the use of timely cues and feedback to encourage habit formation and improved task completion. SmartTask implements this theory through learning-based algorithms, including contextual multi-armed bandits and reinforcement learning, which optimize reminder timing and frequency based on user response and task completion outcomes.

- Adaptive Systems Theory

	- Adaptive Systems Theory underpins SmartTask’s ability to dynamically adjust its behavior using user data. Machine learning and data analytics algorithms—including supervised learning, clustering, time-series analysis, and anomaly detection—enable the system to continuously learn from user interactions and evolve its reminder strategies and productivity insights.

- Integrative Perspective

	- Together, these theories provide the conceptual foundation, while the algorithms serve as the mechanisms that operationalize theoretical principles into system behavior, resulting in improved task completion and time management.

### Conceptual Framework (IPO Approach)

The SmartTask system follows the Input–Process–Output (IPO) model to clearly define how user data is transformed into intelligent reminders and productivity insights. Each stage explicitly incorporates computer science algorithms to justify the system as an algorithm-driven solution.

#### Input (User Data Acquisition)

- The Input stage consists of structured and unstructured data provided or generated by the user:

	- Tasks and task descriptions
	- Deadlines and due dates
	- User-defined priorities and preferences
	- Estimated effort and task categories
	- Historical task completion data

- At this stage, data preprocessing techniques (e.g., normalization of deadlines, encoding of priorities, and feature extraction from user history) are applied to prepare inputs for algorithmic processing.

#### Process (Algorithmic Decision-Making)

- The Process stage represents the core computational component of SmartTask. This stage integrates multiple computer science algorithms under the Intelligent Reminder Engine and Behavior Analysis & Scheduling modules.

##### The following algorithms are applied:

- Priority Scoring Algorithm (Heuristic Optimization)
	- A weighted scoring function computes a dynamic priority score for each task based on urgency, importance, effort, overdue status, and user history.

- Scheduling Algorithm – Earliest Deadline First (EDF)
	- Tasks are ordered using EDF to ensure that tasks with the nearest deadlines are prioritized for reminders.

- Contextual Multi-Armed Bandit Algorithm
	- Used to determine the optimal timing and frequency of reminders by learning which reminder strategies yield the highest task completion probability in different contexts.

- Reinforcement Learning (RL)
	- Models reminder decision-making as a policy optimization problem, adapting reminder strategies over time based on user responsiveness and notification fatigue.

- Completion Probability Prediction (Supervised Learning)
	- Lightweight machine learning models (e.g., logistic regression or decision trees) estimate the likelihood of task completion given a specific reminder time and context.

User task execution and interaction feedback are continuously collected to update these models.

#### Output (System Responses and Analytics)

The Output stage delivers algorithm-driven results to the user through the system interface:

- Intelligent and adaptive task reminders
- Ranked task lists based on priority and deadlines
- Visual productivity metrics and summaries

This stage also applies data analysis algorithms to transform collected data into insights:

- Clustering Algorithms (k-means / hierarchical clustering) to identify user productivity patterns
- Time-Series Analysis (moving averages, exponential smoothing) to track and forecast productivity trends
- Association Rule Mining (Apriori / FP-Growth) to discover patterns between task attributes and successful completion
- Anomaly Detection (z-score–based methods) to detect unusual drops in productivity or spikes in overdue tasks

### Definition of Terms

- Intelligent Reminder – A system-generated notification that adapts based on user behavior.
- Task Completion Rate – Percentage of tasks completed within the scheduled time.
- Productivity Analytics – Visual representation of task performance data.
- Usability – The ease with which users interact with the system.

Chapter 2 aims to:

1. Present existing theories, concepts, and findings related to the study

3. Identify gaps in existing literature and studies
4. Justify the need for the present study

1. Introduction
2. Review of Related Literature
   - Foreign Literature
   - Local Literature
3. Review of Related Studies
   - Foreign Studies
   - Local Studies
4. Synthesis of the Literature and Studies

### Step 1: Introduction of Chapter 2

**What to include:**
- Overview of the chapter
- Importance of reviewing related works
- Connection to the present study

**Example:**

> This chapter presents the review of related literature and studies relevant to the present research. It includes both foreign and local literature and studies that provide theoretical and empirical support to the variables investigated. The synthesis highlights gaps in previous research which justify the conduct of the present study.

### Step 2: Review of Related Literature

> **Literature** refers to theories, concepts, laws, models, and expert opinions.

#### A. Foreign Literature

**What to look for:**
- Definitions of key concepts
- Theories related to variables
- Models and frameworks
- International standards

**How to write:**
- One concept per paragraph
- Start with author and year
- Paraphrase ideas
- Relate to your study

**Example:**

> According to Porter (2018), organizational performance is influenced by leadership style, communication, and employee motivation. He emphasized that transformational leadership improves employee engagement, resulting in higher productivity. This concept supports the present study, which examines leadership style as a factor affecting employee performance.

---

#### B. Local Literature

**What to look for:**
- Philippine authors
- Local laws and policies (CHED, DepEd, DOH, etc.)
- Local books and institutional publications

**Example:**

> Aquino (2019) emphasized that effective classroom management significantly affects student learning outcomes. In the Philippine context, structured classroom routines were found to enhance discipline and engagement. This literature supports the present study focusing on classroom management strategies in public schools.

---

### Step 3: Review of Related Studies

> **Studies** are actual research works such as theses, dissertations, and journal articles.

#### A. Foreign Studies

**What to include:**
- Author(s) and year
- Research design
- Respondents
- Major findings
- Relevance to your study

**Example:**

> Smith and Johnson (2020) conducted a quantitative study on the effects of online learning on student motivation among 300 college students in the United States. Findings revealed that interactive platforms significantly increased motivation. This study is related to the present research as it investigates online learning and student engagement.

---

#### B. Local Studies

**Example:**

> Dela Cruz (2021) conducted a descriptive study on study habits of senior high school students in Manila. Results indicated that time management significantly influenced academic performance. This local study supports the present research examining factors affecting academic achievement.

---

### Step 4: Synthesis of Literature and Studies

**Purpose:**
- Integrate findings
- Compare and contrast studies
- Show trends and patterns
- Relate all to your variables

**Example:**

> Both foreign and local studies indicate that motivation and learning environment significantly affect academic performance. Foreign studies emphasize technology integration, while local studies focus on teaching strategies and classroom conditions. These insights highlight the importance of contextual factors, which the present study seeks to further examine.

# The Input-Process-Output (IPO) Model 

In academic research, the **Input-Process-Output (IPO) model** serves as a foundational conceptual framework used to visualize the flow of a study. Unlike the financial IPO process, the academic IPO model focuses on the transformation of research data into meaningful insights and conclusions. It is particularly effective for experimental, descriptive, and systems-based research where the methodology is central to the study's validity [1].

## The IPO Framework Overview

The IPO model organizes a thesis into three distinct but interconnected components, providing a logical structure for the research methodology.

| Component | Description | Role in Thesis |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Input** | The raw materials, variables, and existing knowledge required for the study. | Defines **what** is being studied (e.g., demographics, independent variables). |
| **Process** | The specific methods, tools, and actions taken to transform inputs into results. | Explains **how** the study is conducted (e.g., data collection, statistical analysis). |
| **Output** | The final results, conclusions, or products generated by the research. | Demonstrates **what was achieved** (e.g., findings, recommendations, new models). |

## Requirements in the Process Section

The **Process** section is the "engine" of the IPO model. For a thesis to be academically rigorous, this section must detail the specific requirements needed to execute the research. These are categorized into **Knowledge**, **Input**, and **System** requirements.

### 1. Knowledge Requirements
This refers to the intellectual expertise and theoretical understanding required by the researcher to perform the process phase effectively.

*   **Methodological Expertise:** Understanding of research designs (e.g., quantitative, qualitative, or mixed-methods) and their appropriate application [2].
*   **Statistical/Analytical Proficiency:** Knowledge of specific analytical techniques, such as **Regression Analysis**, **T-tests**, or **Thematic Coding**, depending on the research type.
*   **Domain-Specific Knowledge:** A deep understanding of the subject matter to interpret data accurately within its specific context.
*   **Ethical Standards:** Knowledge of institutional review board (IRB) protocols, informed consent, and data privacy regulations.

### 2. Input Requirements (Process-Specific)
While the "Input" block of the model contains the variables, the "Process" section requires specific operational inputs to function.

*   **Data Collection Instruments:** Validated tools such as **survey questionnaires**, **interview guides**, or **observation checklists** [1].
*   **Sampling Frame:** A clearly defined group of participants or data sources that meet the study's inclusion criteria.
*   **Raw Data:** The actual responses or measurements collected from the field or laboratory.
*   **Baseline Literature:** Existing studies used for comparison or to justify the analytical framework.

### 3. System Requirements
This encompasses the technological and physical infrastructure needed to manage, process, and analyze the research data.

| System Category | Requirements | Examples |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Data Management** | Secure storage and organization systems for large datasets. | Cloud storage (Google Drive, OneDrive), encrypted hard drives. |
| **Analytical Software** | Specialized tools for quantitative or qualitative data processing. | **SPSS**, **R**, **Python** (for quantitative); **NVivo**, **Atlas.ti** (for qualitative). |
| **Collection Platforms** | Systems used to gather data from respondents. | **Google Forms**, **SurveyMonkey**, **Qualtrics**. |
| **Reference Management** | Tools to organize citations and maintain academic integrity. | **Zotero**, **Mendeley**, **EndNote**. |

# Computer Science Projects Structured with the Input-Process-Output (IPO) Model

The Input-Process-Output (IPO) model is a powerful conceptual tool for structuring and analyzing computer science projects, providing a clear framework for defining system boundaries, required resources, and expected outcomes. Below are three detailed examples of Computer Science projects, with a focus on the **Knowledge, Input, and System Requirements** for the **Process** phase.

## Example 1: Image Classification Model for Disease Detection (Machine Learning)

This project aims to develop a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) model capable of classifying medical images (e.g., X-rays) to detect a specific disease.

| IPO Component | Description |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Input** | Labeled dataset of medical images (e.g., X-rays), pre-processed and normalized. |
| **Process** | Training, validation, and testing of a deep learning model. |
| **Output** | A trained, production-ready image classification model and a performance report. |

### Process Requirements

| Requirement Type | Specific Requirements |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Knowledge** | **Deep Learning Theory:** Understanding of CNN architectures (e.g., ResNet, VGG), backpropagation, and optimization algorithms (e.g., Adam). **Programming:** Proficiency in Python and deep learning frameworks (TensorFlow or PyTorch). **Domain Expertise:** Basic understanding of medical image analysis and common artifacts. |
| **Input** | **Hyperparameters:** Defined values for learning rate, batch size, number of epochs, and loss function. **Model Architecture:** Code defining the layers and structure of the CNN. **Data Augmentation Scripts:** Code to generate synthetic variations of the training data. |
| **System** | **GPU Computing:** Access to a dedicated GPU (e.g., NVIDIA CUDA-enabled) or a cloud computing platform (e.g., AWS SageMaker, Google Colab Pro). **Development Environment:** Python 3.x, Jupyter Notebooks, and a version control system (Git). **Storage:** High-speed storage (SSD) for handling large image datasets. |

## Example 2: Real-Time Inventory Management Dashboard (Web Development)

This project involves building a full-stack web application that displays and manages a company's inventory data in real-time, including low-stock alerts and sales trends.

| IPO Component | Description |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Input** | Raw inventory data (SKU, quantity, location, sales rate) stored in a relational database. |
| **Process** | Developing the backend API, database queries, and frontend user interface. |
